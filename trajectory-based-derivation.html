Trajectory-Based Proof of the Policy Gradient Theorem

Direct derivation of expected return gradients over full episodic trajectories without environment transition derivatives.

What This Proof Establishes

Theorem (Policy Gradient Theorem — trajectory form). For an episodic MDP with parameterized policy $\pi_\theta$, the gradient of the expected return $J(\theta)$ with respect to the policy parameters is:

$$\boxed{\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim p_\theta(\tau)}\left[\left(\sum_{t=1}^T \nabla_\theta \log\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)\right)\left(\sum_{t=1}^T r(s_t,a_t)\right)\right]}$$

In words: the gradient of expected return equals the expected product of (a) the sum of log-policy gradients along the trajectory and (b) the total reward collected along that trajectory. This is an exact equality — no proportionality constants, no approximations in the theorem itself.

Why this matters: it gives us a formula for $\nabla_\theta J(\theta)$ that depends only on quantities the agent can obtain from experience — the policy's own log-gradient and the observed rewards — without ever needing to differentiate through the environment's dynamics. This is the foundation REINFORCE and all Monte Carlo policy gradient methods are built on.

Step 1 — Define $J(\theta)$ Over Whole Trajectories

Define the objective directly as the expected total reward over full trajectories:

$$J(\theta) \doteq \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim p_\theta(\tau)}[r(\tau)] = \int p_\theta(\tau)\,r(\tau)\,d\tau$$

where:

  • A trajectory is $\tau = (s_1, a_1, s_2, a_2, \ldots, s_T, a_T)$ — a complete episode of state-action pairs.
  • The total reward of a trajectory is $r(\tau) \doteq \sum_{t=1}^T r(s_t, a_t)$.
  • The trajectory probability under policy $\pi_\theta$ is:
$$p_\theta(\tau) = p(s_1)\prod_{t=1}^T \pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)\,p(s_{t+1}|s_t,a_t)$$

This is the same quantity as $v_{\pi_\theta}(s_0)$ — "value of the start state" and "expected return over trajectories starting there" are the same number. The difference is bookkeeping: instead of recursing state-by-state through the Bellman equation, we treat the entire trajectory as one object.

Step 2 — The Log-Derivative Identity

The key algebraic fact that does most of the work:

$$p_\theta(\tau)\,\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau) = p_\theta(\tau)\,\frac{\nabla_\theta p_\theta(\tau)}{p_\theta(\tau)} = \nabla_\theta p_\theta(\tau)$$

This is the log-derivative trick applied to the entire trajectory probability (rather than to a single action probability). It lets us rewrite $\nabla p_\theta(\tau)$ — which we can't easily work with — in terms of $p_\theta(\tau)\,\nabla\log p_\theta(\tau)$ — which we can, because $p_\theta(\tau)$ will cancel into an expectation.

Step 3 — Differentiate $J(\theta)$ Directly

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \nabla_\theta \int p_\theta(\tau)\,r(\tau)\,d\tau = \int \nabla_\theta p_\theta(\tau)\,r(\tau)\,d\tau$$

Swap in the identity from Step 2 to replace $\nabla_\theta p_\theta(\tau)$ with $p_\theta(\tau)\,\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(\tau)$:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \int p_\theta(\tau)\,\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau)\, r(\tau)\,d\tau = \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim p_\theta(\tau)}\Big[\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau)\, r(\tau)\Big]$$

No recursion, no unrolling, no auxiliary quantities. We've converted an intractable integral into an expectation — something estimable by sampling trajectories.

Step 4 — Expand $\log p_\theta(\tau)$ and Let Environment Terms Vanish

Take the log of the trajectory probability from Step 1:

$$\log p_\theta(\tau) = \log p(s_1) + \sum_{t=1}^T \log \pi_\theta(a_t|s_t) + \sum_{t=1}^T \log p(s_{t+1}|s_t,a_t)$$

Differentiate w.r.t. $\theta$. Two of the three terms do not depend on $\theta$:

  • $\log p(s_1)$ — the initial state distribution is a property of the environment, not the policy. $\nabla_\theta \log p(s_1) = 0$.
  • $\log p(s_{t+1}|s_t,a_t)$ — the transition dynamics are a property of the environment, not the policy. $\nabla_\theta \log p(s_{t+1}|s_t,a_t) = 0$.

Only the policy term survives:

$$\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau) = \sum_{t=1}^T \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)$$

This is the critical insight of the trajectory route. The environment terms ($p(s_1)$ and $p(s_{t+1}|s_t,a_t)$) are additively separate from the policy term inside $\log p_\theta(\tau)$, so differentiating simply deletes them. The environment's influence on which states get visited never needs to be differentiated — it disappears for free.

Step 5 — The Final Form

Substitute Step 4 back into Step 3:

$$\boxed{\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim p_\theta(\tau)}\left[\left(\sum_{t=1}^T \nabla_\theta \log\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)\right)\left(\sum_{t=1}^T r(s_t,a_t)\right)\right]}$$

This is an exact equality — no proportionality constants, nothing absorbed into a step size. The trajectory-level approach never introduces a stray constant in the first place.

Making it computable. We can't evaluate the expectation analytically (the true distribution over all trajectories is intractable). Instead, sample $N$ trajectories by running the current policy, and use a Monte Carlo estimate:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \left(\sum_{t=1}^T \nabla_\theta \log\pi_\theta(a_{i,t}|s_{i,t})\right)\left(\sum_{t=1}^T r(s_{i,t},a_{i,t})\right)$$

Each sampled trajectory contributes a score (the sum of log-policy gradients) multiplied by its total return. Average over enough trajectories, and the estimate converges to the true gradient — this is REINFORCE.
